Authentication

Picture 4: picture from SecureCoding

Introduction

Login vulnerabilities might seem easy to get, but they can be the most dangerous.

Why? Because logging in is the front door to a website's secrets. If someone sneaky gets past that door, they can cause a lot of mischief. That's why it's essential to know how logins can be tricked and how to make them strong.


What's a Login Anyway?


When you log into a site, it's like showing an ID to prove who you are. The website checks if you're the real deal and then lets you in. This checking process is called "authentication". Since websites are open for everyone to visit, they need a strong bouncer at the door, aka a solid login system.


Types of 'IDs' or Authentication:

Something you know: Like a password or a secret answer. Think of it as a secret handshake.

Something you have: Like a special keycard or a code sent to your phone. Imagine it as a VIP pass.

Something you are: Like your fingerprint or voice. It's like recognizing you by your face or voice.

Login vs. VIP Passes:


Login (Authentication): Proves you are who you say you are.

VIP Passes (Authorization): Determines what you can and can't do once you're inside. For instance, just because you're in a concert doesn't mean you can go backstage, right?

How Can Logins Be Tricked?


Weak Passwords: If passwords are easy, they can be guessed.


Sloppy Code: Sometimes, the way a website is built allows sneaky people to skip the login.

Broken Logins: A website might have a broken door lock, letting people sneak in without the right key.


Why Worry About Login Tricks?

If a bad actor logs in as someone else, they can see and do everything that person can. If they pretend to be the boss of a website, they might control everything. Even if they're just pretending to be a regular person, they might find more doors to sneak through inside.


Examples


1. Guessing the Password:


Scenario:

Imagine you have a diary with a tiny lock. Your friend tries to open it by guessing the lock's code.


In Tech Terms: This is like a hacker trying to get into an account by guessing the password. If it's something simple like "password123", they might get in!


2. Peeking Over the Shoulder:


Scenario:

You're entering the door code at school, and someone's standing too close, trying to see the numbers you press.


In Tech Terms: This is called "shoulder surfing". Someone watches as you type in your password on a computer or phone.


3. Finding a Lost 'Key':


Scenario:

You drop your house key in the park, and someone else finds it and uses it to enter your house.


In Tech Terms: This is like someone finding a USB stick (which acts as an authentication device) and using it to access a computer or system.


4. Impersonating a Friend:


Scenario:

A stranger calls you, pretending to be your friend, and asks for the password to your shared game account.


In Tech Terms: This is a form of "phishing". Hackers pretend to be someone trustworthy to trick you into sharing passwords.


5. Magic Bypass:


Scenario:

Imagine a club with a secret knock to get in. One day, someone finds out they can just clap and get in without knowing the secret knock.


In Tech Terms: Due to a flaw in a website's code, a hacker might bypass the login page altogether without needing a password.


6. Copying the Keycard:


Scenario:

At a hotel, someone secretly scans and copies your room's keycard while you're at breakfast.


In Tech Terms: This is like "cloning". An attacker duplicates a security token or card to gain unauthorized access.


Quick Recap: Just like in real life, there are many ways sneaky folks can try to bypass or trick the ways we prove who we are in the digital world. Always be cautious and protect your 'digital keys'!


Authentication Cheat Sheet for Students


1. Strong Passwords:


What: Use a mix of letters (both uppercase and lowercase), numbers, and special symbols.

Why: It's like having a stronger lock on your diary. Harder for sneaky folks to guess!


2. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):


What: Use more than one way to prove it's you. Like a password PLUS a code sent to your phone.

Why: Two checks are better than one. It's like having a secret handshake AND a password.


3. Don't Reuse Passwords:


What: Keep a unique password for each site or app.

Why: If one site's password gets guessed or stolen, your other accounts remain safe.


4. Know About Phishing:


What: Be cautious of strange emails or messages asking for your password.

Why: Sneaky folks might pretend to be your friend or a trusted company to trick you.


5. Logout When Done:


What: Always log out from sites, especially on shared or public computers.

Why: It's like closing and locking the door when you leave your house.


6. Update Regularly:


What: Keep your software, apps, and devices updated.

Why: Updates often fix "broken locks" or weak spots that sneaky folks know about.


7. Check for HTTPS:


What: When on a website, especially before logging in, check if the URL starts with "https://" and not just "http://".

Why: "HTTPS" is like a secret code between you and the website. Others can't easily listen in!


8. Keep Secrets Secret:


What: Never share your password. Not with friends, not even if someone claims to be from "tech support".

Why: You wouldn't give your house key to a stranger, right?


9. Use Password Managers:


What: Consider using tools that remember your passwords for you.

Why: It's like having a secret vault for all your passwords. You just need to remember one super-strong password for the vault!


10. Stay Informed:


What: Pay attention to news about big websites being hacked. If you have an account there, change your password!

Why: It's like finding out a sneaky person has a copy of keys to many houses. Better to change your locks just in case!


Quick Recap: Protecting your online "identity" is super important. It's like making sure only you and trusted folks can enter your secret clubhouse. Keep your passwords strong, unique, and secret, and always stay alert!
