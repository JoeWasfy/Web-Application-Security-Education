What is Information Disclosure?

Imagine you're playing a game, and you accidentally find a cheat code that reveals secrets about the game. Similarly, in the digital world, sometimes websites accidentally reveal secrets. This is called "information disclosure."


Why is this Bad?


Imagine if your diary got into the wrong hands, and someone read all your secrets. They could misuse that information, right? Similarly, when websites leak information,


bad people can misuse it to harm the website or its users.

Common Ways Websites Leak Information:


1-Verbose Errors: Like a game giving away too many hints.

2-Code Comments: Like finding hints in the rulebook of a game.

3-Hidden Hints: Hidden clues in a game that can be found if you look carefully.

4-Old & Backup Files: Like an older version of a game that still has some cheats.

5-Telling Headers: Like a game's settings menu revealing secrets.

6-Timing Attacks: If a game takes longer to respond to some actions, it might be giving away clues.

7-Unprotected Secrets: Like a secret level in a game that anyone can access.


How Can We Protect Websites from Leaking Info?


1-Be Less Verbose: Don't give away too many hints.

2-Clean the Code: Remove all cheat codes and hints from the game.

3-Protect Secrets: Make sure secret levels are well-hidden.

4-Update & Patch: Always use the latest version of the game, which has fewer cheats.


Remember:


Always protect your secrets, both in games and in real life. If you find a cheat or a secret, report it responsibly and help make the game (or website) safer for everyone!

1. Toy Update without Checking:

Imagine you have a toy that can receive new songs or games from the internet. But, instead of checking if the new songs or games are from the toy company, it just accepts anything. A naughty kid could send a song with bad words, and your toy would play it. This is similar to devices updating without verifying the source .


2. Baking Contest with a Twist:

You're in a school baking contest where everyone gets a secret recipe. A sneaky student finds a way to change the recipes without anyone noticing. On the day of the contest, many students end up with failed dishes because they followed the tampered recipes. This is like the SolarWinds attack where an essential recipe (software update) was altered maliciously.


3. Trading Cards with Hidden Messages:

You and your friends trade cards that have secret codes on them. When you get a card, you read the code and know a secret message. But, a sneaky friend creates a card with a code that, when read, makes you give all your cards to him. This is like Insecure Deserialization - using a coded message to trick the system.
