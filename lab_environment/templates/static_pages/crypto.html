Cryptographic Failures

Picture 5 : picture from freecodecamp.org

What's Cryptographic Failure?

Imagine you have a special digital lockbox where you store things like passwords or credit card details. If this lock isn't strong enough or is broken, bad actors can get in. That's what happens when we talk about cryptographic failures - it's like someone breaking that lock


Common Mistakes:


1-Sending or storing sensitive info without a protective cover.

2-Using old or weak locks (encryption methods).

3-Using easy-to-guess or default keys.


How Can bad actors Use These Mistakes?


1-Reading your info because it's not protected.

2-Breaking weak or old locks.

3-Sneaking in without even breaking the lock, because they know or guess the key.


Top Tips to Stay Safe:


1-Understand Your Data: Know what info is super important and needs extra protection.

2-Limit Data Storage: If you don't need certain info, don't store it.

3-Always Use a Protective Cover: Always keep your data under a protective layer.

4-Stay Updated: Use the latest and best locks.

5-Keep Your Keys Safe: Think of encryption like a key to a box. Make sure it's a unique and strong key.


Detailed Tips:


-When sending or storing data, make sure it's not readable by just anyone.

-Use the newest and strongest methods to lock away your data.

-Don't use the same key everywhere and update your keys regularly.

-Make sure you're using a strong and new method to protect data in transit, like TLS.

-Don't allow your protected data to be stored in caches.

-If passwords are your keys, make sure they're complex and stored securely.

-Always double-check your protection measures. Make sure they're working as intended.


Remember: Your data is like treasure. And every treasure needs a good, strong chest and lock.

Always ensure your locks are the best and keep your keys safe!


Simple Guide to Cryptographic Failures with Examples


What's Cryptographic Failure?


Think of cryptographic failures like having a weak lock on your treasure chest.

If someone knows how to pick or break the lock, they can steal the treasure inside.


Examples :


1. Basic Password Protection :


- What's the issue? Using just a basic lock for passwords without any extra protection.

- Real-world example: A company only scrambles passwords but doesn't add any extra protection. bad actors can use common tricks to unscramble these passwords.


2. Predictable Number Generators :


- What's the issue? Using a machine that creates predictable codes.

- Real-world example: Imagine a lottery machine that always picks similar numbers. If you notice the pattern, you could guess the next number.


3. Broken Trust Links :


- What's the issue? Not checking the entire chain of safety links.

- Real-world example: It's like having multiple doors to a vault, but only checking a few of them. If one is left unlocked, someone can sneak in.


4. Not Fully Securing Communications :


- What's the issue? Using a protective shield, but only sometimes.

- Real-world example: You have a secret chat with a friend in a public place, but sometimes you whisper and sometimes you speak normally. Anyone can overhear the normal chat parts.
